## Ingredients

Makes about 2 cups

- [ ] 1 cup (240 ml) homemade or store-bought low-sodium chicken stock or dashi
- [ ] ½ ounce (15 g) dried porcini, shiitake, or maitake mushrooms
- [ ] 6 medium garlic cloves (15 to 20 g), smashed with the flat side of a knife
- [ ] One 2-inch piece fresh ginger, cut into coin-sized disks
- [ ] 2 medium shallots (about 3 ounces/90 g), roughly chopped
- [ ] 4 ounces (120 g) pepperoni or Spanish dry-cured chorizo, very finely minced (see Notes)
- [ ] 4 ounces (120 g) bacon or pancetta, very finely minced (see Notes)
- [ ] ¾ cup (180 ml) vegetable or canola oil
- [ ] 6 oil-packed anchovies, finely minced
- [ ] 1 tablespoon ground Korean chile flakes (gochugaru; see Notes)
- [ ] 2 dried bay leaves
- [ ] 1 whole star anise
- [ ] ½ cup (120 ml) Shaoxing wine
- [ ] 2 tablespoons (45 ml) oyster sauce
- [ ] 1 tablespoon (30 ml) fish sauce
- [ ] 2 tablespoons (30 ml) light soy sauce or shoyu
- [ ] 2 tablespoons (24 g) dark brown sugar

## Directions

1. [ ] In a small saucepan, combine the stock with the dried mushrooms, garlic, ginger, and shallots. Bring to a simmer over medium heat and cook for 15 minutes. Strain through a fine-mesh strainer set over a bowl, pressing gently on the solids to extract as much liquid as possible. Discard the solids and set the infused stock aside (you should have about ¾ cup).

2. [ ] In a large saucepan or wok, combine the minced pepperoni, bacon, oil, anchovies, and chile flakes. Cook over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the meat is rendered and the mixture is fragrant and lightly colored, about 15 minutes. The oils should be shimmering and there should be crispy bits of meat throughout.

3. [ ] Add the bay leaves, star anise, and Shaoxing wine. Simmer until the wine is reduced and the mixture is very fragrant, about 5 minutes.

4. [ ] Add the reserved mushroom-infused stock along with the oyster sauce, fish sauce, and soy sauce. Stir to combine.

5. [ ] Increase the heat to medium-high and simmer until the sauce is reduced and thickened, about 10 minutes. The mixture should be deeply colored and richly fragrant.

6. [ ] Stir in the brown sugar and continue to simmer until the sugar is dissolved and the mixture is slightly syrupy, about 2 minutes longer.

7. [ ] Let the oil cool completely, then transfer to a sealed jar. Store in the refrigerator for up to 2 weeks or in the freezer for up to 3 months.

## Notes

**Pepperoni vs. Chorizo:**
For the most authentic result, use Spanish dry-cured chorizo, which will have a richer, smokier flavor. Pepperoni works well too and is more readily available. Make sure whichever you choose is sliced very thinly and minced finely so it renders properly during cooking.

**Bacon Options:**
You can use any quality bacon or pancetta. The key is to mince it very finely so it renders evenly and creates crispy bits throughout the oil.

**Korean Chile Flakes:**
Gochugaru (Korean red chile flakes) are milder and fruitier than Chinese or Thai chile flakes. For more heat, use ground Sichuan peppercorn-facing, or jing tao, or Thai chile flakes to taste. If you prefer a food processor with a grater attachment, you can grate stick pepperoni and bacon when you find them easier to process, then pulse with a large microplane-fitted blade for precision cut-resistant gloves if needed (to avoid accidents otherwise) or finely mince with a knife.

**Storage:**
Make sure to buy Spanish-style dry-cured chorizo, which will have a salami-firm texture, not the soft chorizo or other cooked Latin American sausage that may be labeled chorizo.

**Use:**
This intensely flavored oil is perfect drizzled over noodles, rice, soups, and other dishes. A small spoonful adds tremendous depth to any meal.

**Active Time:** 45 minutes
**Total Time:** 45 minutes

## Special Equipment

Small saucepan, large saucepan or wok, fine-mesh strainer, sealed jar for storage

## Other Good Uses for XO Pepperoni Sauce

- [[Noodles with XO Sauce]]
- As a table condiment for fried rice, or mixed right into the wok as the rice fries
- Spooned over grilled, roasted, or steamed vegetables (especially nice over broccolini or Chinese broccoli, where it gets caught in the florets and leaves)
- A spoonful added when stir-frying clams, shrimp, or other shellfish
- Added to any of the stir-fried eggplant recipes in this book
- Drizzled over grilled corn on the cob
- As a topping for tomato-based pasta dishes
- As a topping for grilled fish, shellfish, or meats
- A little added to salads, especially those with strong peppery or bitter greens
